BOARDTAU: A MODERNIZED AND INTERACTIVE 
WEB SYSTEM FOR BOARDING HOUSES NEAR

Background of the Study
With the rapid advancement of information technology, web-based platforms serve as effective tools for addressing real-world problems, including accommodation searching and property management. Current research shows that interactive web-based systems significantly enhance the efficiency of locating rental properties by providing detailed listings, search and filtering mechanisms, and real-time communication features (Alejo D. Bisquera et al., 2026; Monteverde et al., 2023). However, many existing rental platforms primarily focus on commercial hotels or high-end real estate, often overlooking the specific needs of students and university personnel who require affordable, verified, and secure boarding houses options.
Choosing a suitable accommodation is one of the major challenges faced by students and faculty members, especially those who come from distant areas and require temporary or long-term housing near educational institutions. Individuals who study or work far from their hometowns often need accommodations that are affordable, safe, and conveniently located near their school or workplace. Boarding houses, apartments, and dormitories commonly serve as temporary residences and function as a second home for students, providing not only shelter but also access to shared facilities such as bathrooms, kitchens, and laundry areas. Because of this, securing proper accommodation has become an essentia6l necessity for both students and faculty/instructors pursuing education or professional work outside their local communities (Anwar &amp; Purnama, 2022a; Muhammad Akram Hamzah et al., 2022).  According to Bisquera et al. (2026), digital solutions for student accommodation have proven to significantly improve access to relevant housing information and reduce the time, cost, and effort associated with traditional housing searches.
Despite the growing demand for suitable accommodations, the process of finding a suitable boarding house remains difficult and time-consuming. Many end users rely on traditional methods such as door-to-door inquiries, asking acquaintances, or checking through posters and word-of-mouth recommendations. These methods are often inefficient, especially for those unfamiliar with the area, often resulting in wasted time, higher transportation costs, and lack of security leaving tenants vulnerable to rental scams and inaccurate property information. The absence of a centralized and updated source of boarding house information further complicates the search process and may negatively affect academic performance, work efficiency, and overall well-being, as highlighted by Anwar and Purnama (2022) and Hamzah et al. (2022). Similar challenges are observed in other countries where students face difficulties in locating affordable and safe housing without digital support (Bisquera et al., 2026).
Locally, Tarlac Agricultural University (formerly Tarlac College of Agriculture) has implemented an online reservation and monitoring system for on-campus dormitories to improve record keeping and communication among students, parents, and dormitory managers (Parazo, 2016). While this system supports accommodation management within the university campus, it is limited to dormitory use and does not address the needs of students and faculty members who seek off-campus boarding houses near the university. Furthermore, earlier boarding house-related systems previously developed within the institution were largely static and limited in functionality, providing only basic information without features such as real-time room availability, streamlined inquiry submissions, preference-based advanced filtering, identity verification, real-time messaging, or secure reservation payments. These limitations highlight a gap between existing solutions and the current accommodation needs of the university community. This gap highlights the necessity for a web-based platform that provides updated boarding house information and facilitates interaction between students and property owners.
Additionally, boarding house owners face challenges in promoting and managing their properties due to limited access to effective digital tools. Manual promotion restricts visibility and reach, making it difficult to manage room availability, or track business performance. This gap highlights the need for a centralized, interactive, and web-based platform that can improve property exposure, streamline booking processes, and facilitate communication between property owners and potential tenants, ultimately benefiting both end users and property owners (Monteverde et al., 2023).
In response to these challenges, this study proposes the development of BoardTAU, a modernized and interactive web-based platform designed to facilitate the search, booking, and management of boarding houses, apartments, and dormitories near TAU. The system will assist students and faculty in finding suitable off-campus accommodations by providing accurate location information, detailed property listings, real-time room availability, and user-friendly features that enhance decision-making and accessibility. At the same time, BoardTAU supports boarding house owners in promoting and managing their properties efficiently. By integrating modern web technologies and user-centered design, the platform aims to provide a practical, scalable, and effective solution that addresses the common difficulties encountered in traditional boarding house searching and improves the overall accommodation search experience within the university community.

Scope and Delimitations
This study focuses on developing BoardTAU, a modernized and interactive web-based platform designed to facilitate the search and discovery of boarding houses, apartments, and dormitories near Tarlac Agricultural University. The system will provide features such as browsing and searching for available accommodations, applying filters based on price, location, amenities, rules, and room type, viewing detailed property information, identity verification through biometric liveness detection, real-time internal messaging, automated business analytics, submitting inquiries or reservation requests, and providing ratings and reviews. The IT solution will be accessible via any web browser, providing optimized and dedicated user interfaces for both desktop and mobile devices.
The study will focus on boarding houses apartments, and dormitories in Tarlac Province, primarily targeting areas convenient for students and staff near Tarlac Agricultural University. The inquiry process allows users to express interest in a listing, which the landlord may then approve or decline. Upon landlord approval, users may secure their reservation by processing the reservation fee digitally through credit or debit card via Stripe, or through local e-wallets such as GCash and Maya via PayMongo. The system will not cover high-end hotels, commercial transient houses, or properties outside the defined geographic scope. 
Evaluation and testing of the system will involve students, instructors, faculty members, staff, property owners, and IT experts to assess usability, functionality, and overall efficiency.
The duration of the study will be from January 2026 to December 2026, covering development, testing, and evaluation phases.

REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE AND STUDIES
This chapter presents a review of related literature and studies concerning web-based and mobile-based boarding house and accommodation management systems. It examines the transition from manual boarding house management to digital platforms, highlighting the importance of interactive systems in improving efficiency, accessibility, and user satisfaction. By synthesizing local and international studies, related projects, and theoretical foundations, this chapter aims to provide a deeper understanding of how modern information systems address challenges in accommodation searching, reservation, and property management. These discussions establish the foundation for the development of BoardTAU, a modernized and interactive web-based boarding house system tailored to the needs of Tarlac Agricultural University.
In most college towns, finding affordable and convenient boarding houses remains a challenge for student and instructors. Traditional methods, such as physically visiting properties, relying on word-of-mouth recommendations, or maintaining handwritten records, are often time-consuming, inefficient, and prone to errors (Bustillo, 2016; Consignado et al., 2017). These challenges highlight the need for digital solutions that can simplify accommodation searching and management.
Web-based and mobile boarding house systems have emerged as practical tools to address these issues. According to Anwar and Purnama (2022), digital platforms allow users to locate available rooms efficiently through structured databases and categorized listings, reducing the need for physical inspections. Features such as search filters and sorting options further improve decision-making by enabling students to consider price, location, and available facilities before booking (Dwi Kartinah, 2025).
Reservation and booking functions are essential for streamlining operations. Automated systems reduce scheduling conflicts, ensure accurate availability tracking, and enhance communication between tenants and property owners (Muniandy &amp; Zainal, 2025). Similarly, integrating online payment options increases transaction speed, accuracy, and security, while minimizing risks associated with cash handling (Wardana &amp; Widodo, 2026).
Notification features further strengthen system reliability. Email alerts and in-app notifications help reduce misunderstandings between students and landlords, increasing trust and transparency (Mangca, 2023). Security is also enhanced through role-based access control, ensuring that students, landlords, and administrators have appropriate privileges for data management (Saputra et al., 2025; Zendrato et al., 2025). Centralized management of tenant records, reservations, and payments also improves operational efficiency and reduces errors (Eweoya et al., 2025)
Several existing systems provide insight into best practices for boarding house management. Monteverde et al. (2023) developed a Web-Based Rental House Smart Finder, which demonstrated strong usability and security but lacked mobile accessibility and digital payment features. Negara et al. (2021) created a boarding house search system, which offered structured property details but faced cross-browser compatibility issues. Dhamodaran et al. (2021) combined web and mobile platforms with GPS mapping, communication tools, and landlord analytics, though performance and security challenges were noted. BoardTAU addresses these limitations by integrating secure payments, location-based searching, responsive design, and interactive dashboards.
Earlier mobile-based systems also shaped current practices. Consignado et al. (2017) developed HAYBOL, an Android-based apartment locator, improving accessibility through location-based search features. (Nur et al., 2018) implemented automated billing and remote communication using Firebase and MySQL. Guntur et al. (2024) highlighted the value of categorized filters and Google Maps integration in expediting accommodation searches. Location-based technologies, such as Google Maps and the Haversine formula which improves distance accuracy and convenience for users (Amda &amp; Fitri, 2025; Bustillo, 2016)
University-focused systems further emphasize localized solutions. Parazo, (2016) created a dormitory reservation and monitoring system for Tarlac College of Agriculture, improving reservation tracking but lacking mobile optimization. Other studies stress the importance of authentication, role-based access, and mobile-ready platforms to ensure efficient and secure management (Marlia Sari et al., 2024; Oloyede et al., 2024; Pernate et al., 2025).
By learning from these systems, BoardTAU aims to provide a reliable and interactive platform that addresses current challenges in accommodation management. The system incorporates features such as advanced search and filtering for boarding houses, apartments, and dormitories; real-time room availability tracking; inquiry submission and booking management; biometric-based identity verification; real-time messaging via Pusher; secure digital payments via Stripe and Paymongo; optimized and dedicated layouts for both desktop and mobile devices; and automated analytics dashboard for landlords and administrators. These features collectively ensure improved efficiency, usability, transparency, and accessibility for end users, property owners, and system administrators at Tarlac Agricultural University.
METHODOLOGY
System Development Methodology
This chapter presents the methodology used in the development of the IT solution entitled BoardTAU: A Modernized and Interactive Web System for Boarding Houses Near Tarlac Agricultural University. The methodology describes the systematic approach employed by the researchers in designing, developing, testing, and deploying the web system.
The authors choose to apply the Agile Software Development Methodology as the guiding framework for this project. Agile is an iterative and incremental approach to software development methodology that focuses on continuous feedback, adaptive planning, and the rapid delivery of functional system components. According to the Agile Alliance, Agile encourages collaborative development, active involvement of end users, and small, frequent releases that enable progressive improvement of the system.
The Agile method is applicable to BoardTAU because the system needs to constantly improve its key functionalities like advanced filtering, room reservation, payment integration, and real-time notifications. The iterative method allows the researchers to implement functional components of the system over time, incorporating feedback from potential users and stakeholders to ensure that the system aligns with user needs.
By following the Agile methodology, the researchers are able to manage development tasks in a structured phases while maintaining flexibility to accommodate new requirements. This approach ensures that BoardTAU will be able to provide a functional, user-friendly, and secure web platform for the needs of the students, faculty, staff, and property owners of Tarlac Agricultural University.

Brainstorming. In this phase, the researchers conducted initial data collection to identify the needs, preferences, and problems faced by the target users of the system. The IT solution, BoardTAU, was designed to address issues related to finding suitable boarding houses near Tarlac Agricultural University. The researchers first created a survey for students, faculty, staff, and instructors to collect data about their current accommodation arrangements. The survey aimed to understand how the users search for boarding houses, the criteria they considered important when choosing accommodations, and the problems they encountered during the search process. The questions also included items regarding user preferences for amenities, room types, and policies, such as pet regulations, smoking restrictions, and 24/7 security. At the same time, the researchers also conducted interviews with boarding house landlords to identify common challenges in managing properties, marketing problems, and issues related to handling tenant inquiries. This approach enabled the researchers to determine system requirements that would benefit both end users and property owners. The data gathered from surveys and interviews were analyzed to identify recurring patterns, common problems, and preferences. Based on the results of the analysis, the researchers drafted a list of system requirements, including advanced search and filtering options, room reservation functionality, rating and review feature, real-time messaging between tenants and landlords, dual-payment gateway support for both card and e-wallet transactions, and a landlord business analytics dashboard. The output of this phase was a comprehensive list of requirements that would serve as a guide for the design and development stages, ensuring that the system would meet the actual needs and expectations of all stakeholders.
Design. During this phase, the researchers were able to translate the requirements gathered in the brainstorming phase into a detailed blueprint for the BoardTAU interface and system architecture. Wireframes and user interface (UI) designs were developed for each type of user: students, faculty, staff, landlords, and administrators. These designs showcased key pages such as the landing page, search and filter interface, boarding house details page, reservation process, review submission page, and landlord management dashboard. The interface designs were created using Figma, ensuring a modern, consistent, and responsive layout compatible with desktop, tablet, and mobile devices. The system architecture was designed using Eraser.io, while the Input-Process-Output (IPO) diagrams were created using Microsoft Visio to illustrate the flow of user interactions, data processing, and system outputs. Unified Modeling Language (UML) diagrams were also developed to represent the functional interactions between users and the system. These included processes such as searching for listings, filtering results, submitting reservations, managing properties, sending real-time messages, processing payments, and performing administrative tasks. The database schema was designed using Prisma ORM's schema definition language, establishing a single source of truth for all data models including users, listings, rooms, reservations, messages, reviews, and notifications. Security and authentication mechanisms, including role-based access control, one-time password (OTP) verification, AES-256-GCM message encryption, and password hashing, were also planned during this phase. The output of this phase was a complete set of wireframes, UI designs, system architecture diagrams, database schema definitions, IPO diagrams, and UML diagrams. These served as a comprehensive blueprint for the development team in implementing BoardTAU.
Development. In this phase, the researchers transformed the design blueprints into a fully functional web application. Using Next.js with TypeScript, both the frontend and backend components of BoardTAU were implemented, with a focus on responsive design and seamless user interaction. The frontend was built using React 19 with a component-based architecture, styled with Tailwind CSS for responsive layouts, Radix UI and Shadcn for accessible UI components, and Framer Motion for smooth animations and micro-interactions. TanStack Query was utilized for server state management and caching, React Hook Form with Zod for type-safe form handling and validation, and Zustand for lightweight client-side state management. Core features developed included user registration and authentication via NextAuth.js with OTP verification through Resend and React Email, advanced search and filtering for boarding houses, inquiry and booking management, real-time messaging between tenants and landlords powered by Pusher WebSockets with AES-256-GCM encryption for message security, and role-based dashboards for landlords and administrators with analytics charts built using Recharts and Chart.js. On the backend, Prisma ORM was implemented as the data access layer for type-safe database queries against MongoDB Atlas, while Upstash Redis was integrated as a server-side caching layer to optimize performance for frequently accessed data. Payment integration was implemented using a dual-gateway strategy: Stripe for credit and debit card transactions, and PayMongo for local Philippine e-wallet payments including GCash and Maya. Both payment providers utilized webhook-based verification to ensure secure and reliable transaction processing. Location-based features were incorporated using React Leaflet for interactive map rendering on the client side. Identity verification was implemented using MediaPipe for face detection and liveness checking, while Google Generative AI (Gemini) was integrated for intelligent system features. Sentry was configured for real-time error tracking and performance monitoring. Middleware and role-based access control were applied to secure API routes and manage user permissions, with Upstash Rate Limiter protecting endpoints against abuse. Testing was conducted concurrently to verify the functionality of each module throughout the development process. The output of this phase was a fully functional, integrated, and responsive web application that adhered to the design specifications and supported all intended user workflows.
Testing. During this phase, the researchers evaluated the functionality, performance, and reliability of BoardTAU to ensure that all implemented features were working as intended. Functional testing was conducted to validate core system processes, including user registration and authentication, advanced search and filtering, inquiry and booking workflows, real-time messaging via Pusher, payment processing through both Stripe and PayMongo, and role-based dashboard access. Each module was tested individually before performing integration testing to verify smooth interactions between the frontend, backend API routes, Prisma ORM, and the MongoDB database. User Acceptance Testing (UAT) was also conducted with selected students and landlords to assess usability, responsiveness, and overall user experience. Edge cases, input validation, and security features such as role-based access control, OTP verification, AES-256-GCM message encryption integrity, and rate limiting were tested to prevent unauthorized access and ensure data integrity. Any bugs or performance issues identified during testing were documented and resolved iteratively. The outcome of this phase was a stable, secure, and fully functional system with verified workflows, minimized errors, and improved usability, ensuring that BoardTAU was ready for deployment and real-world use.
Deployment. During this phase, the researchers deployed BoardTAU into a production environment. Vercel was used to host both the frontend and backend API routes as serverless functions, ensuring smooth and continuous deployment through an automated CI/CD pipeline integrated with GitHub. The database was configured using MongoDB Atlas as a cloud-based storage solution, Upstash Redis as a server-side caching layer, and EdgeStore for secure file storage including property images, review media, and identity verification documents. Environment variables were properly set up to safeguard sensitive credentials, including API keys, authentication secrets, payment gateway configurations, Pusher connection keys, and encryption secrets. Before going live, the researchers conducted a final series of tests, including database connections, authentication flows, payment processing through both Stripe and PayMongo, real-time messaging via Pusher, email delivery through Resend, file uploads to EdgeStore, and role-based access control in the production environment. Sentry was configured for real-time error monitoring and alerting, while Vercel Analytics and Speed Insights were enabled to track performance metrics and Core Web Vitals. Once all tests were complete and successful, the system was made accessible to its intended users. The objective of this stage was to have a fully deployed, accessible, and functional web system that could process actual user interactions while maintaining security, availability, and performance.

Figure 10.  System Architectural Design
Figure 10 illustrates the complete system architecture of BoardTAU, depicting the flow of data and interactions across seven architectural layers. The process begins when an end user (tenant), landlord, or administrator interacts with the platform through a web browser or mobile browser in the Client Layer. The interface is rendered by the Frontend Layer, built with Next.js 16, React 19, and TypeScript, and styled using Tailwind CSS, Radix UI/Shadcn, and Framer Motion. Client-side state is managed through TanStack Query and Zustand, while React Leaflet, Pusher.js, and MediaPipe handle maps, real-time updates, and face detection on the client side, respectively. All API requests pass through the Middleware/Security Layer, which enforces authentication via NextAuth.js, role-based access control (RBAC) for multi-role authorization, API rate limiting through Upstash Rate Limiter, and cross-origin request security via CORS policies. Authorized requests are then forwarded to the Backend Layer, which serves as the core logic hub. The backend processes requests through Next.js API Route Handlers and Server Actions, accesses the database via Prisma ORM, and manages server-side caching through an Upstash Redis client. Security is enforced at this layer through Bcrypt for password hashing, AES-256-GCM for message encryption with tamper detection, and Zod for server-side input validation. The Data Layer consists of three storage systems: MongoDB Atlas as the primary document database for all application data, Upstash Redis as a key-value cache layer for frequently accessed records, and EdgeStore for file storage including property images, review media, and identity verification documents. The backend integrates with several External Services: Pusher for real-time WebSocket communication (messaging and notifications), Stripe and PayMongo for dual-gateway payment processing (credit/debit cards and GCash/Maya e-wallets, respectively), Google and Facebook OAuth for social authentication, Resend with React Email for transactional email notifications, Google Gemini AI for intelligent features, MediaPipe for liveness detection during identity verification, and Sentry for real-time error tracking. Finally, the entire platform is deployed through the Deployment &amp; DevOps Layer, hosted on Vercel as serverless functions with GitHub-based CI/CD pipelines, and monitored via Vercel Analytics and Speed Insights.

Figure 14.  Entity Relationship Diagram for Room Inventory and Context-Aware Amenities
Figure 14 illustrates the Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD) for the Room Inventory and Amenities domain within the BoardTAU platform. The Room table represents the individual rental units available inside a specific property, connected to the parent Listing table through a one-to-many relationship ("contains"). To ensure granular inventory control, the Room table independently tracks pricing, capacity, and status metrics. Figure 15.  Entity Relationship Diagram for the Booking Lifecycle and User Reviews
Figure 15 illustrates the Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD) for the Booking Lifecycle and User Feedback domains within the BoardTAU platform. The booking lifecycle is carefully managed through two distinct stages represented by separate tables. The Inquiry table records initial tenant applications, securely capturing the student's personal message, biometric identity verification (selfiePhotoUrl, idAttachmentUrl), contact information, and specific occupant role. An inquiry progresses through a strict status flow (e.g., PENDING, APPROVED, REJECTED). Only approved inquiries that have completed the financial requirements produce a Reservation record. To promote transparency and accountability within the community, the Review table collects user feedback tied to a listing and optionally to a specific reservation. Each review captures an overall rating alongside five detailed sub-ratings: cleanliness, accuracy, communication, location, and value. The relationship between the User and Review tables is one-to-many, allowing a student to review multiple different stays over time, while the Reservation to Review relationship is strictly one-to-one optional, enforcing a policy of only a single verified review per completed stay. This ERD segment highlights BoardTAU’s highly scalable, performance-optimized approach to managing the entire tenant lifecycle from initial biometric inquiry to post-stay evaluation.
Data. The system collects and processes various forms of data, including Personally Identifiable Information (PII) and sensitive personal information, to support authentication, inquiry validation, and boarding house management functionalities. The integration of identity verification mechanisms ensures the legitimacy of users and minimizes fraudulent transactions within the platform.
The collected PII includes full name, email address, contact number, home address (city and region), profile images, and emergency contact information. These data are utilized for user identification, communication, and system personalization. Additionally, the system collects sensitive personal information such as government-issued identification cards (e.g., National ID, passport, or driver’s license) and live selfie images used for face verification. These features are implemented as part of a Know Your Customer (KYC)-like process to ensure that all inquiries are valid and submitted by legitimate users. For landlords, before adding a new boarding house listing the system requires submission of legal documents such as business permits, fire safety certificates, barangay clearances, and other supporting documents. These are used by the administrator for validation before a boarding house listing becomes publicly available in the platform. All collected data are stored using a combination of a MongoDB database and a secure file storage system implemented through EdgeStore. The system utilizes three distinct storage buckets with defined access control mechanisms. The public storage (publicFiles) is used for non-sensitive files such as profile images, boarding house listing images, room images, and other publicly displayed media. These files are accessible to all users to support platform functionality such as browsing and viewing property listings. However, upload operations require authenticated access, and file management is restricted through controlled deletion permissions. The media storage (reviewMedia) is used for images and videos associated with property listings and user-generated reviews. These media files are uploaded by tenants after their stay to document and share their experiences in a boarding house, including visual feedback such as room conditions, facilities, and overall accommodation experience. These files are publicly accessible to support transparency and user decision-making within the platform. In contrast, the private storage (identityDocs) is used for highly sensitive files such as identification cards, biometric selfies, and legal documents. This storage implements strict access control mechanisms, ensuring that only the file owner and authorized administrators can access the files. Access is enforced through role-based authorization and user ownership validation to prevent unauthorized viewing or retrieval of sensitive documents. To ensure data security, the system implements multiple protective measures, including password hashing, encrypted data transmission, role-based access control (RBAC), and secure file handling. Uploaded sensitive files are stored in protected storage environments with restricted access, while authentication mechanisms ensure that only authorized users can perform data operations. Furthermore, the system collects operational data such as chat messages, inquiry details, reservation details, booking details, timestamps, and administrative activity logs. These are used for communication, transaction tracking, and system monitoring. Only necessary and relevant information is displayed in reports to maintain data minimization and system efficiency. For system validation and testing, sample boarding house data including simulated room rates, dummy images, and test reviews were used to evaluate core functionalities such as the search engine, Haversine distance filtering, and booking processes. These datasets consisted entirely of simulated records and did not contain any real or confidential user information.
All data collection and processing activities are conducted in compliance with the provisions of the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (RA 10173). The system ensures that personal data are collected for legitimate purposes, securely stored, and accessed only by authorized personnel, thereby maintaining confidentiality, integrity, and user privacy. The researchers further ensure that data handling practices are limited to academic and system evaluation purposes, and that all collected information is protected through appropriate security and access control mechanisms to prevent unauthorized disclosure.
Safety and Risk Management. The system implements comprehensive safety and risk management measures to ensure the protection of user data, system reliability, and secure operation of all platform functionalities. Since the platform handles sensitive information such as government-issued identification cards, biometric selfies, legal documents, and user communication data, appropriate safeguards are applied to minimize potential privacy, security, and operational risks.
A key risk addressed in the system involves unauthorized access to sensitive user data, particularly identification documents, biometric images, and other confidential files. To mitigate this, the system enforces layered security mechanisms that combine role-based access control (RBAC) and user ownership validation. These mechanisms ensure that access to private resources is strictly limited to the file owner and authorized administrators, thereby reducing the risk of unauthorized retrieval or exposure of sensitive information.
The system also considers risks associated with identity verification processes, such as face recognition and liveness detection used during the inquiry procedure. Since these processes may be vulnerable if implemented solely on the client side, the system incorporates secure authentication workflows and server-side validation support to strengthen verification integrity and reduce the likelihood of fraudulent submissions.
In relation to communication features, the system recognizes potential privacy risks in user-to-landlord messaging. To address this, access to messages is restricted through authenticated sessions, ensuring that only the involved parties can view conversation content. This helps maintain confidentiality and reduces the risk of unintended information disclosure.
The system also manages location-based data carefully to uphold privacy standards. Precise geolocation data is used only for boarding house listings where necessary, while general user profiles are limited to broader location information such as city and region. This approach follows data minimization principles and reduces unnecessary exposure of sensitive location details.
To further enhance system security, strict validation rules are enforced for file uploads, including file type restrictions (e.g., images for profile and listing content, and images/PDFs for legal documents) and file size limitations to ensure system stability and prevent malicious uploads.
Additionally, the system implements complementary security practices such as password hashing, encrypted data transmission, and secure authentication mechanisms to further strengthen data protection across all modules.
For system reliability and data integrity, proper database management and backup strategies are utilized to reduce the risk of data loss and ensure continuity of operations. Input validation is also consistently applied to prevent malformed or malicious data entries.
Overall, the system is designed and implemented in compliance with the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (RA 10173). It ensures that all collected personal data are processed for legitimate purposes, stored securely, and accessed only by authorized personnel. These combined measures ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the system while effectively mitigating potential operational and security risks.
